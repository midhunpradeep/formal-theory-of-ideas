Scientific Discovery as Recursive Modal Projection: A Deep Research Survey

Cognition as an Interface: Structured Projection with Loss

Contemporary theories across cognitive science and philosophy converge on the idea that perception and thought do not mirror an objective world in full fidelity, but instead project structured representations that sacrifice detail for usefulness. Donald Hoffman’s Interface Theory of Perception epitomizes this view: our perceptions are like a computer’s desktop interface – shaped by natural selection to hide the complex “truth” and only display what aids adaptive behavior (The Interface Theory of Perception - PubMed). In Hoffman’s words, “our perceptions of space-time and objects have been shaped by natural selection to hide the truth and guide adaptive behaviors. Perception is an adaptive interface.” (The Interface Theory of Perception - PubMed) This implies a lossy compression of reality: we only perceive a filtered structure of the world, much as an icon hides the circuitry inside a computer (The Interface Theory of Perception - PubMed). Philosophers Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela similarly argue that an organism “does not see a world ‘out there’ that exists apart from us. Rather, we see only what our systemic organization allows us to see. The world merely triggers changes determined by the system’s own organization. The world cannot instruct an observing system; [instead] the world is constructed by the observing system.” (Maturana | Harish’s Notebook - My notes… Lean, Cybernetics, Quality & Data Science.) In this view, the mind is a structure-determined system (Maturana | Harish’s Notebook - My notes… Lean, Cybernetics, Quality & Data Science.), continually “bringing forth a world” through its embodied activity (Maturana | Harish’s Notebook - My notes… Lean, Cybernetics, Quality & Data Science.) – effectively an interface between sensory inputs and internal models.

This interface perspective is reinforced by Daniel Dennett’s notion of “real patterns.” Dennett proposes that what we call “reality” is composed of patterns that our minds capture via compression: “A pattern exists in some data – is real – if there is a description of the data that is more efficient than the [raw data] bit map.” () In other words, we latch onto information that can be economically described (for some observer), ignoring the rest as “noise.” Cognition, from this standpoint, is sensitive to certain structural regularities while discarding vast details – much like a lossy projection that preserves only salient structure. This explains how different observers or models can carve up reality differently, depending on what they compress or ignore () (). Even our folk psychological narratives about beliefs and intentions can be seen as projecting a simplified, definite structure (“hard edges”) onto the underlying neural complexity () – a point Dennett connects to how language (a mode of expression) imposes apparent definiteness on inherently fuzzy mental processes ().

Cognitive scientists have formalized these ideas in the brain’s functioning. R.L. Gregory famously described perception as a process of unconscious hypothesis testing. He wrote that “Perceptions may be compared with hypotheses in science. The methods of acquiring scientific knowledge provide a working paradigm for investigating processes of perception.” (Perceptions as hypotheses - PubMed) The brain’s sensory pathways are not passive channels but actively interpret signals based on prior knowledge and assumptions, just as scientists interpret data via theories (Perceptions as hypotheses - PubMed). Systematic perceptual errors (illusions) are “important clues” that reveal the brain’s built-in assumptions, much like failed hypotheses in science reveal where our theory is wrong (Perceptions as hypotheses - PubMed). This aligns closely with modern predictive processing models, which conceive the brain as a recursive prediction engine. In these models, the brain constantly generates a “mental model” to predict incoming sensory signals and then adjusts itself based on the prediction errors (Predictive coding - Wikipedia). The guiding principle – often cast in Bayesian terms – is to minimize surprise or loss (e.g. Friston’s Free Energy Principle), highlighting that cognition is organized around error/loss functions guiding the update of our “interface” representations (Cognitive Filtering of Physical Law: An Evolutionary Perspective on Human Perception and Formulation of Physical Principles | by Boris (Bruce) Kriger | THE COMMON SENSE WORLD | Mar, 2025 | Medium) (Cognitive Filtering of Physical Law: An Evolutionary Perspective on Human Perception and Formulation of Physical Principles | by Boris (Bruce) Kriger | THE COMMON SENSE WORLD | Mar, 2025 | Medium). In short, a broad range of thinkers (from evolutionary psychology to neuroscience) support the notion that cognition is a lossy projection of reality: we perceive and think in terms of user-friendly icons, hypotheses, or patterns that are optimized for utility or fit (in an evolutionary or pragmatic sense) rather than veridical detail (The Interface Theory of Perception - PubMed) (Maturana | Harish’s Notebook - My notes… Lean, Cybernetics, Quality & Data Science.).

Recursive Cycles of Reasoning and Discovery

The process of scientific discovery itself has long been described as iterative and recursive. Classical philosophy of science views inquiry as a cycle of conjecture and test. Karl Popper’s famous schema of “conjectures and refutations” portrays knowledge growth as an unending feedback loop: bold hypotheses are proposed, deduced for consequences, then rigorously tested in attempts to refute them; those that survive are provisionally retained, and the cycle repeats (Conjectures And Refutations Summary PDF | Karl Popper) (Conjectures And Refutations Summary PDF | Karl Popper). As a summary puts it, “scientific knowledge evolves through a cycle of conjectures and refutations… bold and imaginative guesses about the nature of reality [are] then rigorously tested… through this relentless cycle of proposing and discarding hypotheses, science advances” (Conjectures And Refutations Summary PDF | Karl Popper) (Conjectures And Refutations Summary PDF | Karl Popper). This self-correcting recursion ensures that each loop (hypothesis → experiment → outcome → revised hypothesis) refines our theories, much as each iteration of a learning algorithm reduces error. The hypothetico-deductive model formalizes a similar loop: formulate hypothesis, deduce predictions, collect observations/experiments, then update or reformulate hypotheses (Hypothetico-deductive model - Wikipedia) (Hypothetico-deductive model - Wikipedia).

Notably, Charles S. Peirce anticipated these ideas in his 19th-century logic of science. Peirce identified abduction, or inference to a hypothesis, as the crucial leap in inquiry – “the only logical operation which introduces any new idea” (Abduction through entanglement - The Cynefin Co). In Peirce’s triadic cycle, abduction generates a new plausible idea to explain surprising facts, deduction works out its testable consequences, and induction evaluates those consequences against experience, leading either to the hypothesis’s provisional acceptance or to another cycle of abduction (Abduction through entanglement - The Cynefin Co). This recursive loop (often depicted as a cycle of hypothesis generation → experimentation → evaluation) closely mirrors the phases outlined in “recursive modal projection”: generation, observation, evaluation, refinement, memory. Each phase can be seen as a mode of cognition that transforms the content and feeds into the next. For example, generation (abductive mode) produces an idea, observation mode gathers data under a given framing, evaluation compares outcomes to expectations (inductive confirmation or falsification), refinement adjusts the idea (or generates new ones) based on that feedback, and memory stores the knowledge for future cycles – thereby influencing the next generation phase.

(File:The Scientific Method.png - Wikimedia Commons) Illustrative schematic of an iterative inquiry process. This simple flowchart shows a recursive loop in the scientific method: starting with a Question and background Research, a Hypothesis is formed, an Experiment is conducted to gather Data, leading to a Conclusion that feeds back to new Questions. Each loop of this process corresponds to transforming ideas through different modalities – hypothesizing, observing, analyzing – akin to the modal phases of recursive modal projection. Such diagrams emphasize that scientific reasoning is not a linear path but an ongoing, cyclic feedback system (Scientific method - Wikipedia) (Scientific method - Wikipedia). In practice, scientists refine their hypotheses repeatedly (“iterate”) in light of new data, embodying recursive abstraction refinement. Philosopher Daniel Dennett has even compared this to evolutionary algorithms: the mind can generate many “variations” of ideas and then selectively retain the ones that work, an analogy originally formalized by Donald Campbell. In 1960, Campbell proposed that “creative thought should be conceived as a blind-variation and selective-retention process (BVSR)” (Creative thought as blind-variation and selective-retention). In this evolutionary epistemology, each new hypothesis is a random variation and experimental testing acts as a selection mechanism, filtering out the failures – effectively a recursive generate-and-test loop driving discovery. Modern psychology of creativity and problem-solving echo this: for instance, the Geneplore model (Finke, Ward & Smith) describes creative cognition as alternating between generative phases (producing ideas or “preinventive structures”) and exploratory phases (evaluating and elaborating those structures), cycling until a satisfactory outcome emerges. Douglas Hofstadter likewise underscores the iterative nature of high-level cognition: he famously argued that analogy is “the core of cognition”, with thought continually mapping previous structures onto new situations to spawn novel insights (Analogy - Wikipedia). Every analogy or metaphor is a mini abductive leap – a projection of a known structure onto a new context – which is then assessed and refined. This fits neatly into a recursive loop model: analogies generate candidate understandings, which are then evaluated and either confirmed, adapted, or discarded, looping until the problem is solved.

Crucially, these cycles are self-referential and abstraction-driven. Each complete loop can enrich an evolving body of knowledge (memory), which then informs the next iteration. As Peirce noted, reasoning is “a sort of dialogue” the mind has with itself, where each phase’s outcome becomes the input (or premise) for the next. This resonates with later cybernetic models of learning (e.g. the OODA loop in decision theory: Observe–Orient–Decide–Act, continually repeated). It also parallels how AI systems refine models: for example, a modern AI might generate an initial model, test it against data, compute a loss (error), and then update the model – a recursive optimization cycle conceptually similar to scientific inquiry. In sum, there is broad support (from Peirce and Popper through modern cognitive science and AI) for treating knowledge-seeking as an iterative, modal process in which different cognitive modes (hypothesizing, experimenting, etc.) feed into each other in a closed loop (Scientific method - Wikipedia) (Conjectures And Refutations Summary PDF | Karl Popper).

Related Paradigms in Cognitive Science and AI

The theoretical framework of “recursive modal projection” finds parallels in several interdisciplinary domains. In enactive cognitive science, Varela and Maturana’s work on autopoiesis and structural coupling posits that an organism’s cognitive apparatus and its environment are locked in a continuous loop of mutual specification. This means the organism enacts a world through perception-action cycles, rather than passively reflecting a pre-given world (Maturana | Harish’s Notebook - My notes… Lean, Cybernetics, Quality & Data Science.) (Maturana | Harish’s Notebook - My notes… Lean, Cybernetics, Quality & Data Science.). Each perception-action cycle can be seen as a traversal through modalities (sensing, moving, adjusting internal state, etc.), and the organism’s history (its memory of interactions) biases what it brings forth next. This aligns with the idea that each cognitive “phase” transforms content and passes it on: in enactivism, “living itself is an act of cognition” (Maturana | Harish’s Notebook - My notes… Lean, Cybernetics, Quality & Data Science.), and the loop of sensorimotor engagement is inherently recursive. In a similar vein, extended mind theories (Clark & Chalmers) and second-order cybernetics (von Foerster) acknowledge that what we call “cognition” often involves iterative interaction with external structures (instruments, media, notations) – essentially projecting mental structure outwards and then perceiving it anew. David Kirsh describes how humans “project” mental structures onto physical substrates and then read them back to amplify thinking (Microsoft Word - cogsci_projection_final_new_ending1.docx) (Microsoft Word - cogsci_projection_final_new_ending1.docx), an interactive loop of externalization and re-internalization. Such observations support treating phases like external observation and internal generation as integrated parts of one recursive cognitive system.

In artificial intelligence, researchers have explicitly modeled multi-phase reasoning loops. The field of computational scientific discovery (pioneered by Herbert Simon and Pat Langley in the 1980s) built AI programs like BACON, GAUSS, and KEKADA that simulate the cycle of discovery – generating hypotheses from data regularities, testing against experiments (simulated data), and refining the hypotheses. These systems showed that alternating phases of proposal and critique can rediscover scientific laws. More recently, advanced AI systems employ meta-learning or self-refinement loops. For example, large language models like GPT-4 can be configured in a reflective loop where one module proposes an answer and another evaluates and revises it, iterating until consistency is achieved ([2501.02026] Recursive Decomposition of Logical Thoughts - arXiv) (Recursive Intelligence Artificial Intelligence (RIAI): The Future of …). Similarly, AlphaGo’s self-play is a form of recursive improvement (each game’s outcome updates the policy for the next game), and automated scientists like the robot scientist “Adam” form hypotheses about gene functions, design experiments, and iteratively update their hypotheses – a literal hardware instantiation of the recursive discovery cycle. In machine learning, the idea of abstraction levels is often handled by hierarchical models: e.g., deep neural networks propagate information through layers (each layer transforming representations), which can be seen analogously to modalities feeding into one another. Furthermore, abstraction-first approaches (like program synthesis or symbolic regression in scientific ML) generate high-level conjectures and then test them on data, mirroring the generate-and-test modality interplay. It’s notable that modern AI research on reasoning is rediscovering the value of recursion: techniques like Tree of Thoughts and ReAct (Reason+Act) involve models iteratively reasoning step-by-step and evaluating partial solutions, which strongly resembles the theoretical phases of generation, evaluation, refinement.

The concept of an “interface of ideas” could also draw on analytic philosophy. For instance, Hilary Putnam’s and Douglas Hofstadter’s reflections on analogy and conceptual mappings highlight that we understand new domains by projecting structures from familiar domains (with inevitable loss of detail). Each such analogical mapping is evaluated and possibly refined or rejected – again an internal loop. Even the semiotic process described by Peirce (sign → interpretation → new sign) is an infinite recursive loop: semiosis never fully resolves, it keeps generating new interpretants (analogous to refined hypotheses). This underscores that any representational system is involved in a recursive interpretation of itself, much as the paper’s framework suggests the phases are unified in a recursive system.

Formal Models and Frameworks to Augment the Theory

To further formalize and visualize recursive modal cognition, we can draw on mathematical and computational frameworks:

  • Category Theory: As a “mathematics of structure,” category theory has been applied to cognitive science to model compositional and hierarchical relationships. Phillips and Wilson (2016) propose a category-theoretic account of systematicity in cognition, wherein cognitive capacities are composed via universal constructions. Notably, they argue that “every universal construction can be viewed as the optimal construction in the given context (category). From this view, [such constructions] are derived from learning, as an optimization.” (Frontiers | Systematicity and a Categorical Theory of Cognitive Architecture: Universal Construction in Context) In other words, finding a universal construction is akin to solving a constraint optimality – strongly echoing the idea of projection with a loss function (the construction is optimal given a context). This hints that each modality in our cognitive loop might be represented as a functor or mapping between categories (e.g., a hypothesis space and an observation space), with loss minimization corresponding to universal property (optimal mapping). Higher-order categories (categories of categories) are suggested for modeling how context itself might evolve (Frontiers | Systematicity and a Categorical Theory of Cognitive Architecture: Universal Construction in Context), capturing the self-referential aspect of a system that can alter its own modes. The Memory Evolutive Neural System (MENS) of Ehresmann and Gómez-Ramirez is another categorical model: it uses colimits (a category theory concept) to characterize how mental objects emerge by iterative binding of neural assemblies (Conciliating neuroscience and phenomenology via category theory - PubMed). Thanks to the degeneracy of neural codes (multiple different neural microstates realizing the same mental state), MENS can explain how higher-order cognitive structures form with flexible implementation (Conciliating neuroscience and phenomenology via category theory - PubMed). This degeneracy is essentially a formalization of losing certain details while retaining structure, enabling general concepts – and is “essential to explain phenomena like productivity and systematicity of thought.” (Conciliating neuroscience and phenomenology via category theory - PubMed) By mathematically distinguishing the neural (micro-level) and mental (macro-level) dynamics, MENS provides a rigorous way to unify different modalities (perception, memory, etc.) in one framework. Intriguingly, Ehresmann and Gómez-Ramirez note that such a formalism “allows developing the naturalized phenomenology approach asked for by Varela (1996) which seeks mutual constraints between lived experience and cognitive science” (Conciliating neuroscience and phenomenology via category theory - PubMed), fulfilling in mathematics what the theory of recursive modal projection aims for conceptually: a bridge between subjective cognitive phases and objective structural modeling.

  • Dynamical Systems: Another modeling avenue is to view the entire cognitive loop as a single dynamical system – a set of state variables (representing the contents of each modality or memory) that evolve over time according to coupled differential (or difference) equations. Each “phase” of the discovery process could correspond to a sub-dynamics or attractor in this system. Dynamical systems theory has been used to explain cognitive phenomena like rhythmic cycles of attention, decision-making oscillations, and learning as trajectory wandering in a complex energy landscape. A famous example is Tim van Gelder’s argument that cognition might be akin to a Watt governor (continuous feedback loop) rather than a discrete computational algorithm. In our context, one could imagine a phase space where each point encodes (for instance) the current hypothesis, expectations, and stored knowledge, and the system’s laws cause it to spiral through “generation→observation→evaluation→refinement” in a continuous loop. Fixed points of this system would represent stable discoveries or beliefs, and limit cycles could represent regularly updating knowledge (as in periodic hypothesis revision). Such a view could be made concrete with neural dynamics models – e.g. recurrent neural networks that implement a form of scientific reasoning by having internal cycles that settle into states (hypotheses) that best predict inputs (data), reminiscent of predictive coding networks minimizing error over time. Tools from dynamical systems (Jacobians, Lyapunov functions) might then be used to analyze stability of knowledge or the sensitivity (chaos) of the discovery process to initial conditions (prior knowledge).

  • Computational Architectures and Simulations: We can also look at cognitive architectures like Soar, ACT-R, or OpenCog, which often have iterative perceive–decide–act loops. For example, Soar breaks problem-solving into sequences of decision cycles and subgoals, where impasses trigger new hypothesis generation (a bit like abduction) and learning mechanisms store the results. This resonates with the modal phases: impasse = need for new generation; state update = observation; operator selection = evaluation; chunking = memory storage. A formal mapping could potentially be made between these architectures and the recursive modal model (each phase as a function from one representational state to the next). Additionally, Markov decision processes (MDPs) and Bayesian networks offer formal means to represent iterative refinement: one could model the state of scientific understanding as a belief distribution that gets updated (Bayesian inference) with each new piece of evidence (observation modality) and occasionally expanded (proposal of new hypothesis priors) when anomalies are high. The loss function idea surfaces here as well – e.g. the negative log-likelihood of observations given the current model can serve as a loss to minimize. Recent AI research on automated theorem proving and program synthesis is also relevant: these systems use heuristic search through an abstract space of possible solutions, often employing recursive strategies (like divide-and-conquer or iterative deepening) to find a proof or program. They sometimes visualize reasoning as a search tree that is explored and pruned, which is analogous to navigating a network of ideas with backtracking – a process not unlike a scientist exploring hypotheses and ruling them out. Insights from these algorithms (like methods to avoid getting stuck in local optima, or to balance exploration vs exploitation) could inform the “refinement” modality of the model.

  • Visualization and Schematics: Conceptually, the recursive modal projection framework could be illustrated by a nested loop or spiral diagram. One might depict five circles or nodes (for generation, observation, evaluation, refinement, memory) with arrows indicating the flow from one to the next in sequence, but also a larger arrow curling from memory back to generation to indicate recursion (the next cycle starts where the last left off). Additionally, each node could be drawn as an operator that takes an input and yields an output (e.g., generation: takes stored knowledge + curiosity → yields new hypothesis; evaluation: takes prediction + data → yields a judgment or error signal; etc.). This would highlight the modularity of each phase yet show the compositionality when linked in a ring. In fact, category theory could provide the rigor for such a diagram: one could model each cognitive modality as a morphism (with an associated functor capturing its loss function or optimization criterion), and the composition of these morphisms around the loop as the overall cognitive process. A fixed-point of the composite (a point where running through all modalities returns you to the same idea state) would represent a fully refined idea that no longer changes – perhaps an analog to a final “discovery” that gets committed to long-term memory and is no longer contested.

Finally, it’s worth noting that the philosophical lineage of these ideas spans centuries. From Kant’s insight that our mind contributes forms to experience (imposing structures like space, time, causality – effectively a projection) to Hegel’s dialectical cycles of thesis-antithesis-synthesis (a recursive progress of ideas), many frameworks have viewed knowledge as actively constructed via iterative interplay rather than passively received. The recursive modal projection model situates itself in this lineage, and the sources gathered – from Peirce’s logic to modern AI – provide a rich scaffolding to support and extend it. Each source offers a piece of the puzzle: why cognition must be lossy (to be tractable and fitness-enhancing), how iterative loops drive learning (ensuring continual self-correction and innovation), and how we might formally capture such a system (through mathematics of mappings, dynamics, or computations). Taken together, these references both validate the framework’s premises and suggest pathways to refine it into a more unified theory of scientific discovery and creative cognition.
